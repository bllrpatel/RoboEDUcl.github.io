RoboEDU

An Introduction to Computational Thinking

The Need for RoboEDU

According to research done by the University of Washington and the Bureau of Labor Statistics, 71 percent of all future STEM jobs will be "computer occupations". After computer occupations, the next highest occuring job will be in the field of engineering at 15 percent. No other field even tops 4 percent. This shows a drastic change in the current and future job market. But what are we doing to prepare the youth of our nation for this change?

Only 5 percent of United States high schools teaches computer science, or has a robotics program. The state of computer science education is abysmal in the U.S., and as such students are not prepared to enter careers in the fi eld of computer science. Additionally, in the near future almost every fi eld ranging from biology, fi nance, to anthropology will incorporate computer science and computational thinking.

To address this issue, CityLab has designed a four part module which incorporates various topics from human physiology, computer science, to introductory robotics. This module serves as a platform to inspire and peek the interests of students who think the field of engineering and computer science are out of reach. The first three modules will be done in a rotation style, each of which will last approximately 50 minutes. The last module will be executed after lunch, which will last approximately 1.5 hours.

The Effects of Nicotine and MSG on Neural Activity

Students will inject nicotine and MSG into a cricket and observe the corresponding neural response.

Measuring Reaction Time to Auditory and Visual Triggers

Students will be conducting an experiment to measure whether they react faster to auditory or visual signals. Additionally, half of the students will test their dominant hand and the other half will test their non-dominant hand to see if their is a significant difference.

Light Controlled Robot

Students will learn simple circuit design and will be introduced to computational thinking, a precursor to computer science. They will then apply their skills by designing a circuit and programming a robot to move with the direction of a light.

EMG Controlled Robotic Vehicle

In a culminating project, students will integrate what they have learned throughout the day and build a robotic vehicle controlled using 4 EMG sensors on 4 different students. An additional two students will be needed to fire a small weapon, and direct the other 5 students.
